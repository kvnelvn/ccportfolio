The Eye of Agamotto

The best way to represent time is through the Time Stone itself. It's quite a terrible way to keep time, because as this would indicate: time was manipulated to bring the apple back to its original state before it was eaten. However, I can live knowing that the first thirty seconds of a minute, time moves forward in a way that is familiar to us. It's only what comes after, for the remainder of the minute, that time is manipulated and it feels like we're only going backwards.

Design Process

The process for this was simple. It all boiled down to coding 6 different versions of my apples and utilizing "if/else if" statements to specify what time intervals will draw what apple. The remainder of the assignment was added fun, bringing together the "Time Stone" theme.

90% of this assignment was coding the apples. I used a series of curveVertex points to draw a more natural feeling shape. I originally intended on making the curves look perfect, but soon realized the imperfections an apple has. Therefore, I ditched the "perfectionist" look and decided to go for something that had irregular curves to show off the natural form of an apple better. I did the "first" layer as the red portion of the apple, then added the "yellow" layer as the flesh. There are certain points that are a simple copy+paste from the red to yellow portions, because this allowed the curves to line up perfectly. I repeated this for 6 different apples, each one editing the points through trial and error to see which curves best suited the images. After the apples were done, it became a matter of writing out the "if/else if" statements. I used second() as the standard of time. I utilized && and || to state what second intervals I wanted each specific apple to appear - there were six total apples, so I used 5 second intervals to so each apple could appear for ten seconds within the minute.

I ended up choosing the apple sketch because it was the one that would challenge me the most. The other sketches felt too abstract; for me, I wanted to convey time through something that was somewhat grounded in reality. Many of the examples of "time" that were shown to us were very abstract.

As the other two sketches demonstrate, abstraction through geometric shapes or lines was something that I considered. After going over the three sketches, I came to the conclusion that it would be a more creative exercise to go for the apples. It was an idea that I had from the very beginning, and I wanted to run with it. And that I did.

What am I reflecting on?

Rubber duck. This was the assignment that really taught me the importance of rubber-ducking your code. And wow, did it help. Coding all the curveVertex points out loud not only helped me understand where they needed to go, but I was able to mentally visualize it as well. In addition, by speaking the "if/else if" statements out loud, I was able to better understand what needed to be written out. It really put into perspective of how coding is just another language that I have to learn; and that the way we code is very much the way we interact in real life. At the end of the day, I designed a hell of a clock. Well, sort of. A clock that doesn't conform to the standard notion of "time." But it's no matter! I learned the best usage of "if/else" statements and learned to also manipulate other areas of code to reflect what I want.
