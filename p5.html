An Optical Illusion

This optical illusion pattern is based off of a wallpaper that I originally wanted to purchase for my own apartment. I found this pattern to be a great example of an "optical illusion", as the way the squares are all laid out can create a sense of discomfort and confusion to look at. We were asked to pick a pattern that was also part of my culture. I chose not to find a pattern that pertained to my Asian heritage, as I spent the majority of my life growing up surrounded by "American" cultures. As a result, I chose something that felt very classicly "American" to me. The original sketches showed a mid-century pattern, and an art-deco pattern.

I decided to go with the mid-century modern pattern, because it was the one that resonated with me the most. I've spent many years falling in love with mid-century designs, creating mood boards for mid-century interiors, and buying different mid-century furnitures to furnish my apartments. Needless to say, mid-century (are we tired of me saying this yet?) is very much an integral part of my design and artistic ethos.

The way I went about coding this was pretty simple actually. I treated each "cube" as its own entity, so I created a user-defined function and drew the different rectangles in there. In addition, I wanted to animate a part of it, so I did a forLoop to create a group of circles that fit into one of the squares. I then took this user-defined function and did a forLoop, with different x-axis points, in order to fully take up the entire canvas.

On This Week's: Reflection

This type of pattern is not uncommon. But, what I wanted to do was translate the aesthetic of a mid-century modern design into this type of optical illusion pattern. By imparting mid-century color palettes and aesthetics, I was able to transform a common optical illusion into something that felt more like an art piece. It retains the vintage and warm feel of a mid-century design, but still has contemporary lines and shapes. The addition of the animating circles is meant to combine contemporary animations with mid-century modern textures.

Recontextualizing this pattern in a digital format is very interesting. Mid-century patterns exist in a physical context i.e. wallpapers, textiles, rugs, etc. So to take the physical and recontextualize it into the digital feels like I'm creating another format in which this iconic design philosophy can exist. I even added a shaded animation in order to, metaphorically speaking, bring it to life. Kind of cool, right? I have mid-century in my home, and now I have mid-century in my portfolio.
