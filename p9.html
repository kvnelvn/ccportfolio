Predator FRIES

A Consentful Interface

"Follow the F.R.I.E.S., and you will get a consentful interface." And so I did. That's the entire premise of this interface. Let's take a look.

(F)reely given: I designed the interace to be without pressure, force, or manipulation. What it is is what it is. There's no hidden layer, double-crossing of the user, or gaslighting. I clearly state the intention of why the camera's used and that everything stays with the user. It gives power to whoever is using it.

(R)eversible: it is always a priority that I have the option to opt out of using the camera. It's not enough to simply have it; the option needs to be easily accessible. I give a clear prompt that the user can opt out using a button, and I made that button easy to see and access. No digging through pages and pages of settings.

(I)nformed: the interface informs the user what their camera is going to be used for. It also gives the user a privacy statement, letting them know that nothing is being recorded and saved. Straight to the point and simple. But effective.

(E)nthusiastic: there's nothing in the interface that forces the user to give their camera access for the sake of using the Predator Cam. In fact, there's also no pressure for them to return if they opt out. The language is purposefully neutral versus overly excitable or manipulative. It's meant to let the user decide on their own.

(S)pecific: specificity is another important aspect of this interface. Like I mentioned, I made it specific, clear, and straight-to-the-point when it came to the camera's purpose. There isn't any hidden meanings or layers behind it.

Well? Tell me! How'd You Do It?

Great question. I started with switch statements - they set the foundation of what the interface would look like, both on the front and back end. Once I knew how many scenes I needed to have, I began to work on the visuals. This was a fun assignment because I got to learn CSS code as well. With the DOMs and buttons, I customized these visual elements through style.css rather than through p5js. This gave me more freedom to assign different looks to different buttons without taking up too much space.

The buttons were customized with CSS through style.css. There, I coded its characteristics, such as padding, background color, font, and even gave it hover and shadow interactions. I took some inspiration from Google's material design in order to incorporate a more "friendly" look and feel to it. Some UI fun, I guess?

Finally, I put everything together in the scenes. Everything came from user-defined functions, and was called in the draw function in the switch scenes. The overall interface has a clean and friendly aesthetic, just to give off a less intimidating vibe to go along with the concept of consentual design. I will say, I tried my damn hardest to write out code that turns off or hides the camera (especially that damn LED) when the camera's not being used, but could not figure it out. Well, something for the next iteration.

Reflect! Reflect!

The F.R.I.E.S. model is very important to follow, especially when you're designing something that other people will be using. I've started to really think about what makes design consentful; but moreso than that, what makes it equitable. A million-page Terms and Conditions in 1px font that's incredibly difficult to find isn't consentful; sure you "give permission", but it's more than just that. This assignment really helped me to think through that lens. Cool shit.
