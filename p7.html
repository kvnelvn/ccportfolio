My Nightmarish Autobiographical Game

To state the obvious: yes, this game is heavily inspired by P.T. In fact, you can argue that it's a 2D top-down rendition of P.T. I remember first playing it; the one game that has truly terrified me to no end. It's been years since I played it (no thanks to Konami for removing it from existence). I look back to the game and think about how it so accurately portrayed nightmares, madness, and even mental health. Which brings me to the autobiographical part of this game.

This game represents the struggle I once had with my own mental health. More specifically at times when it felt like my mind was spiraling into darkness, no matter how hard I tried to get out. Hence, why I decided to make this game feel like P.T., because to me that game represents what it's like to be stuck and helpless, all while the world around you spiraled and got darker. During some of the darker periods of my depression, I would find myself stuck in a "limbo" where I couldn't really get out of an episode. I had all the help I could get, but I still couldn't escape. In the game, this is represented by the seemingly endless hallways and loops that the character goes through. Each time, it only seems to get more nightmarish and mad. It's a pretty terrifying rendition, but the metaphor is there to represent something that's truly terrifying and real.

The Endless Hallway

The idea of the endless hallway (other than being inspired by P.T.) is to represent an endless struggle. Trying to get out and escape with no luck, only to be driven more mad. This gameplay mechanic is the exact thing I knew I wanted to design from the very conception of the idea. The greybox of the hallway is, visually, very simple. As seen below, it's essentially two rectangles to represent the negative space, outside of the hallway, and the character.

The, very long, code for my game can be accessed here: https://editor.p5js.org/kvnelvn/sketches/y14Eb4_ME

In fact, the gameplay mechanics were extremely difficult to code out. Making sure collision detection was coded correctly took weeks to do. On top of that, there was a lot of sound that was added; each sound had to be triggered by the character's x and y position. In addition, there was also triggering the "enemy" of the game, Lisa. It was a matter of importing a lot of different assets, giving them their own functions, and calling them to appear at exact moments.

Just like P.T., there's an endless corridor that the player has to navigate. I wanted to take that design element and incorporate a version of that into my 2D game world. There's two sides of my story in this: one lies within the deeper, more personal aspect regarding my mental health and how this game can portray that. The other is just how moved I was by P.T. when I first played it. I can honestly look back at that time and say that it was that game that inspired me to pursue design through the horror lens.

The Horror Experience

This game had to be horror. It had to be scary. It had to be an absolute maddening nightmare. After all, it did represent a dark side and dark time of my life. Audio plays a big role in horror, and as a result, also plays a big role in my game. I have to give credit to all the various artists I sampled noises, soundtracks, and music from, so that I could bring the experience together.

On top of the audio and visual, the overall gameplay was designed with horror in mind. It's purposefully confusing and frustrating; yes, you are supposed to question whether or not you're even making progress in the game. So, as you continue down each hallway and "advance" each level, I am purposefully making you confused and frustrated and question everything. Because that's how it was like, for me, to deal with a worsening anxiety and depression.
