The Lovecraftian Three

Well, the exquisite corpse resulted in something H.P. Lovecraft couldn't even dream of: an obscurely cute and horrific being so otherworldy that it conjures existential dread, while also being extremely cuddly.

The Being

There's no name for this creature; in fact, I cannot describe it, no matter how hard I try. Nor do I know what it wants? Does it feel? Does it have good or bad intentions? Does it even have any intentions? The night I saw The Being, I was out in the sea. The waters were calm and the air thin. Out of nowhere, a groaning came from deep within the waters, as if the sea let out a cry. Slowly, the water began to tremble more and more. Clouds rolled over turning the sky dark indigo. An eruption from the water shook the earth with rage. And out comes The Being.

What can I say about this Being? The terror I felt, first and foremost. But, then I felt an urge to embrace the Being with intimate love. It wasn't anything I've ever felt. No. It was a new experience. But, it felt nostalgic as well. How can I both wish for death, but feel passionate toward this thing? Curiosity overtook me, and I steered my boat forward to observe, knowing that I would be going forward into a direction with no chances of return. Seconds go by, and I stood in front of the Being. Butterflies in my stomach. Knees trembling and feeling weak. If I felt such fear, why did I also feel joy? A paradox of emotions raged within me, just as the sea itself raged with the Being. Is this my Hell? Or is this my Heaven? Perhaps it's both, and perhaps it's neither. The intense oxymoron of emotions tore me apart. Literally.

I speak to you through the conscious of the Being. My soul and mind seemingly merging with the Being, as my body and flesh fills the wooden floor of my boat. I am the Being. The Being was always me. And what of you, who is reading my thoughts? Are you, you?

Design Process

It's interesting doing this through code versus on paper. I've done this exercise before; where I partnered up with another person and drew the top half, and them the lower half. Fast forward three years, and I'm doing this through code. And I treated this assignment the same way; do one body part at a time.

I was partnered with Duo and Tong, both of whom shared some lovely and detailed sketches. Tonally and thematically, they could not have been more opposite of each other. Duo's sketches had a very "kawaii" look to it, and Tong's felt very lovecraftian (I immediately thought of Cthulu). I took inspiration from Junji Ito when I did my sketches. Bringing these three sketches into one exquisite corpse was going to result in a beautiful and very odd looking thing. And that was my driving factor for the assignment.

There were many elements of the sketch that was comprised of the shape basic function, such as rect() or ellipse(). Naturally it made sense to create a user-defined function with its own set of parameters and arguments to better organize the code. Unfortunately, I had to utilize a lot of curveVertex() so those I couldn't define under a function. However, I still kept an element of modularity and reusability through the remainder of the user-defined functions.

I Look Into a Mirror and What Do I See? My Reflection

This felt like the first assignment; it felt long and tedius having to draw the sketches through code, when I could've easily recreated it through the classic pen and paper. But it was a fantastic exercise because it helped me be more confident in writing functions, vertex points, and all the other goodies packaged in this lovely code. Everything I've learnt so far has been used in one form or another in this assignment, which is pretty cool to see (and even cooler to implement). Would I do exquisite corpse again, through code? Probably not.
